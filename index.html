One War
One War

There were only 21 years between the end of WW1 and the start of WW2. These years were filled with civil wars, revolutions, and border conflicts which were linked causally to both world wars — which themselves were made of many smaller wars that did not all start and end at the same time.

So treating them, like we do, as discrete events separated by a stretch of peace is no more than a convention. It would be equally accurate to talk about one war lasting between 1914 and 1945.

Except, the end of WW2 was also the start of the Cold War (which was not all that cold for Vietnam or Korea). We say that the latter ended with the fall of the Soviet Union, but the news from Ukraine suggests otherwise.

The flow of violence has been uninterrupted in the past 100 years. In some periods, it was more like a giant waterfall, and in others, like a thousand smaller rivers. But it never dried up. And if we look at the wars preceding and leading up to WW1, we’ll see that it did not originate in the 20th century, either.

Perhaps there is just one war, going round and round, and local escalations in different regions of time and space are like solar flares or storms in the ocean — not in any way separate from the currents around them.

I watch from above two lives play out in a sequence, and I hear a continuous, iterative articulation of what these lives are about. Maybe 80% is indistinct, a kind of glossolalia, but occasionally words and phrases emerge out of it. It sounds a little like chanting and works a little like writing a song: a magic search for meaning by touch, through humming, mumbling, stumbling towards something true, a closer and closer articulation of what these lives I am watching play out are expressions of, the principle behind them.

The first life is of a man who builds a house and makes it a home for him and his woman. They share what seems like a good, harmonious life. Later the wife dies, and he carries on, kind of stoic, alone, in the now-empty home.

The second is of a giant tree, like a sequoia, that leads an unrushed, majestic existence for a very long time and eventually gets chopped down. The stump is made into a beautiful house, which later becomes a home, with a warm light of life inside it, thus carrying the tree’s essence — a kind of a calm, dignified beauty — further in time.

I watch this, and it’s resonant. Both lives have a lot of grace to them. There are things in life we must accept with humility and a certain kind of reverence (birth, death, heartache, deep love). These lives are like this. I watch it all like a David Lynch movie, a Buddhist khural, or a psychedelic vision — it speaks to me, I sense that it’s saying something true about reality. The longer I observe, the more precise and generalized the audible articulations of the lives’ methods become. They move to higher levels of abstraction, at each step expressing something more universally true about the two lives and about life itself.

I don’t remember the phrases, but the visions feel so compelling I almost can’t help but claim them as my other lives — past, or adjacent — a mythological or archetypal dimension of the same process that my waking life is another dimension of.

And it’s not threatening. It’s like the way I relate to Tibetan practices or to writing: a reality emerges from my mind and soul through playful, careful engagement, I observe and relate to it, I experience it, and it’s like what I’ve been experiencing, something I’ve always sensed about life. It feels good.

I wake up in a good mood, with a touch of a cheerful, humorous wisdom.
